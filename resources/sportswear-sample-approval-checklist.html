Buyer checklist

Sportswear Sample Approval Checklist

Approve the sample as a controlled production reference: identify the revision, measure it, test the fit and function, compare every visible detail, and close open questions in writing.

Discuss a SampleTech Pack Checklist

Direct answer

What should a buyer approve before bulk sportswear production?

Approve the exact sample revision, material and color references, measurements and tolerances, wearer fit, construction, logo execution, labels, packaging, and every written correction. A visual "looks good" message is not enough when the tech pack, sample, artwork, or comment sheet still disagree.

Identify

Confirm the style code, colorway, size, sample stage, revision, date, and the files used to make it.

Measure

Compare every required point of measurement with the approved method, value, and tolerance.

Wear and inspect

Review fit, movement, coverage, comfort, construction, function, artwork, and visible quality.

Record and close

Write exact changes, assign owners, update the specification, and state whether the sample is approved, revised, or rejected.

Sample stages

Know what each sample is expected to prove

Factories and buyers may use different stage names. Define the deliverable and approval purpose instead of assuming the label means the same thing to everyone.

Seven checks

Review the sample in a fixed order

Use the same sequence for every revision so a visible improvement does not distract from an unresolved measurement, material, or packing issue.

  1. Confirm identity and revision

    Match the physical sample label to the style code, size, colorway, revision, date, tech pack, artwork files, and comment sheet.

  2. Verify materials and trims

    Compare fabric, lining, mesh, elastic, pads, zippers, drawcords, thread, labels, and colors with named approved references.

  3. Measure before judging fit

    Use the documented point-of-measure method. Record actual values rather than describing a garment only as tight, loose, short, or long.

  4. Test fit and function

    Use the intended wearer and activity where practical. Review movement, coverage, support, recovery, closures, pockets, and comfort.

  5. Inspect construction

    Check seam type and position, stitch appearance, reinforcement, symmetry, hems, edge finish, panel alignment, and internal workmanship.

  6. Compare artwork and branding

    Verify artwork revision, size, color, orientation, distance from seams, registration, labels, hangtags, and any sponsor or player data.

  7. Approve packing and handoff

    Confirm folding, barcode and size labels, polybag, insert, assortment, carton marks, open issues, and the final bulk-production reference.

Approval language

"Approved with comments" needs a precise boundary

Some comments can be verified through a revised file or measurement record; others change fit, material, construction, color, or appearance and should be confirmed on a new sample. State which path applies.

  • Approved: the named sample and files can become the production reference with no unresolved product changes.
  • Revise and resubmit: listed changes must be made and shown in a new sample or agreed approval artifact before bulk.
  • Rejected: the sample does not establish a usable production reference; the next revision should not inherit its approval.
  • Open commercial item: quantity, price, payment, packing allocation, or delivery terms remain separate from physical sample approval.

Bulk handoff

Create one production source of truth

Before bulk begins, consolidate approval into a controlled package. Do not make the production team reconstruct the decision history from chat messages.

Approved physical reference

Identify the exact sample retained by the buyer, supplier, or both, including style, size, colorway, revision, and date.

Released specification

Issue the current tech pack, bill of materials, measurements, tolerances, construction, artwork, labels, and packaging files together.

Closed comment log

Mark every comment approved, superseded, rejected, or still open, with the file or sample that proves closure.

Change-control rule

Require written approval before a bulk material, trim, measurement, artwork, label, or packing detail differs from the released reference.

FAQ

Sportswear sample approval questions

Can a sportswear sample be approved from photos?

Photos and video can confirm visible details, but they cannot fully verify wearer fit, handfeel, stretch recovery, comfort, internal construction, or physical measurements. Define which items can be approved remotely and which require a physical reference or other agreed evidence.

How many samples are needed before bulk production?

There is no universal number. The required stages depend on product complexity, new materials, fit risk, size range, artwork, packaging, and how much changed after each review. Approve against evidence, not a target sample count.

Can the bulk material differ from the sample?

Only through a documented and approved change. Record the bulk material reference, explain the difference, and decide whether a swatch, test, component sample, or new garment sample is required before production.

What if the launch deadline is close?

Reduce open variables rather than leaving them ambiguous. Use available materials, focused colors, proven construction, simpler branding, or staged packaging where appropriate, but keep the final approved scope explicit.
