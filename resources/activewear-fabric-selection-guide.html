Direct answer

How should a buyer specify activewear fabric?

Record the fabric identity, composition, construction, finished weight, width, stretch direction, stretch and recovery behavior, color reference, surface finish, opacity requirement, care condition, decoration compatibility, test evidence, and approved physical swatch. GSM is useful, but it cannot define softness, compression, breathability, recovery, or durability by itself.

Published July 15, 2026For leggings, sports bras, training wear, teamwear, and active layersReview material options

Identify the material

Use a supplier or mill code, approved swatch reference, composition, knit or weave, color, finish, and revision.

Describe behavior

Define stretch direction, recovery, coverage, support, drape, airflow, surface feel, and the intended wearing condition.

Set evidence

Name the sample check, wash condition, comparison method, test report, or market requirement that supports each important claim.

Control approval

Link the approved swatch and sample to one dated record, then define how bulk fabric will be checked against it.

Fabric record

Specify more than fiber content and GSM

Two fabrics can share a composition and nominal weight but behave differently because of yarn, construction, finishing, dyeing, width, and production variation.

Selection workflow

Choose fabric from the product task

Start with the wearer and garment function, then narrow materials through physical comparison and controlled samples.

  1. Define the wearer and use

    Record activity, climate, duration, movement, skin contact, coverage, layering, target customer, and intended market position.

  2. Define the garment job

    State whether the fabric must provide support, drape, ventilation, warmth, abrasion resistance, print clarity, or a specific surface feel.

  3. Separate requirements from preferences

    Mark non-negotiable performance and compliance needs separately from preferred handfeel, appearance, or commercial targets.

  4. Request coded swatches

    Compare identified swatches rather than unnamed images. Keep each code connected to composition, construction, weight, width, finish, color, and availability notes.

  5. Test the fabric in the garment

    Review opacity, stretch, recovery, seam behavior, decoration, comfort, and appearance in the actual pattern and intended wearing condition.

  6. Approve one controlled reference

    Release the swatch code, color reference, sample revision, specification, open exceptions, and evidence together.

  7. Define bulk verification

    State which identity, appearance, dimension, performance, and documentation checks apply before cutting and during final inspection.

Translate the brief

Replace adjectives with a reference and acceptance rule

Words such as soft, premium, supportive, squat-proof, quick-dry, or durable communicate direction, but they do not tell a supplier how to reproduce or approve the result.

  • Soft: provide an approved swatch and describe face, reverse, finish, drape, and skin-contact expectation.
  • Supportive: define the garment zone, fit intent, stretch and recovery behavior, lining, elastic, and wearer check.
  • Opaque: define the garment, color, extension or movement condition, lighting, and approval method.
  • Quick-dry: identify the claimed use, comparison or test method, care condition, and evidence required before publishing the claim.
  • Durable: identify the expected use, contact area, washing condition, appearance change, and relevant test or wear trial.

Approval control

Keep swatch, sample, specification, and bulk material connected

Approval is strongest when every reference points to the same material identity and revision.

Swatch comparison

Check face, reverse, color, texture, weight, width, stretch, recovery, and finish against the labeled reference.

Garment validation

Confirm fit, coverage, movement, seams, decoration, labels, care, and appearance using the intended fabric.

Evidence review

Verify that reports identify the tested material, color or lot where relevant, method, date, result, and claimed requirement.

Bulk release

Define incoming checks, approved range, escalation rules, retained reference, and who can authorize a substitution.

FAQ

Activewear fabric selection questions

Is higher GSM always better for activewear?

No. GSM records fabric weight, not overall quality. A useful choice also depends on structure, yarn, finish, stretch, recovery, coverage, drape, care, climate, product pattern, and intended use.

Can composition and GSM identify an approved fabric?

No. Different fabrics can share those two values. Use a coded physical reference plus construction, width, finish, color, behavior, and approval evidence to control the material more reliably.

Should fabric be approved before the fit sample?

Material direction should be established early because fabric behavior affects pattern and fit. Final approval should use the intended material or clearly state which fit, color, performance, or care decisions remain unproven.

How should a fabric substitution be handled?

Treat it as a controlled change. Record the new code and reason, compare specification and evidence, review cost and availability, remake affected tests or samples, and obtain written approval before use.

What should be checked again for a reorder?

Confirm the current fabric code, color, finish, width, weight, approved range, availability, compliance evidence, sample reference, care result, and any lot or process change that could affect the product.
